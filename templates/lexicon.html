Background

The traditional method for sentiment analysis employs sentiment dictionaries to classify document level polarity

It employs human annotated sentiment dictionaries that have preassigned sentiment scores for nouns and adjectives. General sentiment dictionaries such as the Harvard-IV and domain specific dictionaries like the Loughran and McDonald (finance/economics) have been constructed. Words are classified as either positive or negative. After prepocessing text with tokenization, stop word removal, and stemming, the tone of a document is determined by dividing the difference of the positive and negative words over the total number of classified words. This technique is accessible and often implemented by novices. However, due to its simplistic nature, it is only moderately effective. This approach can only classify document level sentiment and relies on human annotated dictionaries to perform lookups of words. It also ignores the nuances of human language. It cannot consider words in context or identify sarcasm, irony, or negation. The inability of the dictionary method to identify these key aspects of writing hampers its accuracy in practice.
