Welcome to SentAI

An Artificial Intelligence Sentiment Analysis Project

About this Project

This Project Explores the Applications of Convolutional Neural Networks for Sentiment Analysis Tasks

This work is the culmination of a year long senior thesis for attainment of a Bachelors of Arts in Computer Science from The School of Computing and Engineering at Quinnipiac University. The project has been developed by Philip Caldarella and advised by Professor Jonathan Blake.


Purpose

OBJECTIVE: The aim of this project was to determine if Convolutional Neural Networks can more effectively derive sentiment from text than the traditional models.

Details: Sentiment analysis has become a key subset of natural language processing. Various approaches have been employed to capture sentiment from text with high levels of accuracy. The traditional lexicon-based approach has proven moderately effective but differs widely by application. The accuracy of this method varies extensively due to the reliance on dictionaries with pre-assigned sentiment values. Domain specific lexicons tend to yield higher levels of accuracy but are limited in scope to the field the dictionaries capture and a loss of word context. Advancements in the accessibility of machine learning technology, namely neural networks, offer a modern and more effective approach. This project trained convolutional neural networks for sentiment analysis utilizing the corpora Stanford Large Movie Review Dataset and the Financial Phrasebank with the PyTorch framework. The objective of this study was to compare the accuracy of convolutional neural networks in deriving document level sentiment to a traditional lexicon-based approach and neural bag of words benchmark.

Results

Key Findings: Results indicate that Convolutional Neural Networks are capable of reaching higher levels of accuracy than traditional approaches

Details: The CNN models outperformed the Dictionary and NBoW baselines. The Stanford Large Movie Review and Financial Phrasebank datasets reached a classification accuracy of 92% and 86% on validation data, respectively. Using macro averaging, the precision and recall metrics were calculated. The CNN models achieved the highest F1-Scores which indicates their ability to correctly predict all classes.

For binary classification, the CNN trained on the Stanford Large Movie Review achieved 91% and 92% accuracy at predicting the positive and negative class, respectively. For multiclass classification, the CNN trained on the Financial Phrasebank predicted the positive, neutral, and negative classes correctly 95%, 68%, and 96% of the time, respectively. Despite lower effectiveness at classifying neutral sentiment, the model still eclipsed the Neural Bag of Words baseline by multiple percentage points for each class.

Conclusions & Next Steps: Despite a high computational cost involved in training, convolutional neural networks consistently outperform traditional approaches to sentiment classification. CNNs ability to consider contextual information and automatically learn meaningful features set it apart from traditional methods which treat each word independently. These models capture the nuances of language like negations, sarcasm, and idioms which leads to a higher probability of correct classification. Results also indicate that CNNs are more robust and capable of generalizing effectively to unseen data. A key next step for this project is to integrate the X API into the web application. The ambition is to build a tool for users to quickly survey sentiment for a topic of their choosing utilizing data pulled directly from X.
